For Jean Baudrillard, a simulacrum is a copy without an original — a sign or image that has replaced reality with a simulation. In the postmodern world, representations (media, consumer goods, digital content) no longer reflect reality but mask its absence, creating a "hyperreal" state where simulation feels more real than the actual world.

Four phases of the image (Baudrillard's progression):
1. Reflection of reality — a faithful, "sacramental" image
2. Masking of reality — a perversion that hides the truth
3. Absence of reality — the image pretends to be a copy but masks that nothing is behind it
4. Pure simulacrum — the image bears no relation to any reality; it is its own self-referential model

The vault connection: the question is whether a vault that accumulates framings of the self (rather than copying the self) is a simulacrum — and if so, which phase. The answer may be that it aims for phase 1 or a well-bounded phase 2, not phase 4.

*Source:* Baudrillard, Jean. *Simulacra and Simulation* (1981). Encountered via journal entry prompted by conversation with Peter.
