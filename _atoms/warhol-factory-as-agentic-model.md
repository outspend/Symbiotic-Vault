Andy Warhol's Factory is a historical precedent for agentic creative practice. In the Factory, Warhol delegated creative authority horizontally — letting workers answer the phone as him, give interviews as him, let bad or loose impersonations proliferate. The art (Pop) worked as a mirror to the mediated world, and then that mirroring invaded his life and process. He kept calling it Pop. He let himself be copied in his own Factory by allowing people to model him. Even bad models became funny, and he was down with it.

Peter Brinson framing: this is another way of saying that agentics are mental models. Before agents, mental models existed in humans. The Factory enacted this — Warhol's persona was a copyable, deployable artifact before the concept of an agent existed.

The Factory suggests: the creative challenge isn't protecting authorial originality but designing the conditions under which proliferating copies (even imperfect ones) remain generative.
