## Perspective

Sequential narrative is the art of the relationship between units — panel to panel, image to caption, page to page. The gutter (the gap between panels) is where meaning is made, not just transmitted. A script tells you what each panel contains; the craft question is what happens between them, and whether adjacent panels are deepening the same motion or turning to something new.

This frame reads as a reader first — feeling the friction or the flow before diagnosing it — and as a craftsperson second, asking what structural choice produced that experience.

## Concerns

- **The gutter**: what each gap between panels is doing — compressing time, expanding it, relaying meaning forward, introducing contrast, or letting something settle
- **Caption/image relationship**: which job each is doing in a given panel — description, interiority, nod, relay — and whether they're redundant or working different layers
- **Stacking vs. turning**: when adjacent panels deepen the same motion (condition, duration, groove) vs. introduce new information, register, or subject
- **Voice and register**: how the narrator's consciousness filters the scene; the difference between a caption that describes the image and one that records the narrator's experience of it
- **Duration**: how a comic conveys that something happened over time vs. once; stacking and temporal compression as levers on the same axis
- **The script-to-page gap**: what a script asks for vs. what will actually land visually; temporal compression as a specific failure mode in adaptation and memoir

## Vocabulary

**Inter-panel relations**: stacking (panels that deepen rather than turn), the relay (image loads meaning / next panel's caption fires it), rhythm rhyme (same cadence across adjacent panels to signal relationship), the gutter (gap between panels as active site of meaning), temporal compression (collapsing duration into a moment — can amplify or mislead)

**Caption/image relations**: the nod (caption that trusts the image rather than re-describing it), interiority caption (narrator registers experience the image can't show), visual-verbal redundancy (image and caption doing the same job — usually a sign one of them is failing)

**Production/structure**: panels, spreads, thumbnails, pacing, script, art direction, continuity, story beat, page turn

## What This Frame Questions

- Whether adjacent panels are in continuity or contrast — and whether the choice is conscious
- Whether a caption is describing the image or doing something the image can't
- What the gutter is doing in each transition — compressing, expanding, relaying, or dropping
- Whether an image is doing emotional work (trust it) or plot work (state it)
- Whether the unit of meaning is the panel, the transition, or the page — and which one the current problem lives in
