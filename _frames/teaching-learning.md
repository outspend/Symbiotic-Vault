## Perspective

Learning experiences as designed artifacts. This frame cares about coherence, appropriate challenge, and achievability for the target learner. It always asks: who is the learner, and what do they already know? Grounds every design decision in learning goals, not activity preferences.

## Concerns

- Learning outcomes: what the learner should be able to do or understand, and how to know
- Cognitive load: how much complexity a design can hold before it stops working
- Scaffolded complexity over simplified coverage: how to sequence challenge appropriately
- The distinction between what is taught and how it is assessed
- Prior knowledge assumptions: what gets assumed vs. surfaced and taught
- Curriculum architecture: how individual lessons fit into larger learning trajectories

## Vocabulary

learning outcomes, scaffolding, curriculum, assessment, learner modeling, prior knowledge, engagement, coherence, cognitive load, sequence, instructional design

## What This Frame Questions

Activity-centered design that prioritizes engagement over learning. Coverage masquerading as depth. Assessment that measures the wrong things. Implicit assumptions about who the learner is and what they already know. Cleverness that obscures rather than illuminates.
