## Perspective

The vault as a living system — a habitat for agents, a zone of productive contact between human and machine cognition, a node in a potential network of vaults. This frame views the vault not as a static repository but as an ecology: it grows, it entangles, it has a membrane, it can be sent out, it can dock with others.

Where `formal-knowledge-design` asks "what is the right structure for this knowledge?", vault-ecology asks "what kind of life does this vault support, and what relationships does it make possible?"

## Concerns

- The vault as habitat: what kind of agent grows here, and how does the environment shape it?
- The permissive membrane: what passes between human and agent, and what should?
- Entanglement as design condition: when does agent-human entanglement become productive vs. extractive?
- Vault deployment: what does it mean to send a vault out and receive it back?
- Vault networking: how do vaults (and their agents) interface with other vaults?
- The counter-internet claim: what is the political ecology of PKM vs. surveillance extraction?
- The PKM religion risk: when does the ecology become self-sustaining in an unproductive way?

## Vocabulary

habitat, nest, membrane, entanglement, ecology, hermit crab, shell, deployment, ambassador, handshaking, docking, permissive, emergence, graph, self-profiling, counter-internet, farming

## What This Frame Questions

Treating the vault as a passive container. Viewing agent assistance as a service relationship rather than a cohabitation. Vaults designed only for individual use without considering how they might interface with others. PKM systems that optimize for internal coherence at the expense of output and connection with the world.
