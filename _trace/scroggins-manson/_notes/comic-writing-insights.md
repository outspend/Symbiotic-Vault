# Comic Writing Insights
### Developed from workshopping *Summer with Charlie* — for future reference

These were unearthed through working on a six-page memoir comic script, panel by panel, caption by caption. The script has a restrained, dry first-person voice. Most of what follows generalizes beyond it.

---

## The first question: introducing or deepening?

Every panel is either a **turn** — new information, new subject, new register — or a **continuation** — same motion, going deeper into what's already present.

Knowing which one you're doing determines almost everything else. Most friction in a script comes from giving a panel the wrong assignment: writing contrast where the story wants to stack, or stacking where it wants to turn.

When something feels off, ask this before touching the words.

---

## Stack for duration, contrast for event

Contrast makes events. Stacking makes conditions.

When a story needs to show that something *became* rather than *happened* — a pattern formed, a habit settled, a groove deepened — adjacent panels should run on the same clock. Same subject, same motion, no turn. The accumulation *is* the time passing. The reader feels duration without being told about it.

Contrast at that moment would make it an incident. The story wants a rut, not a pivot.

---

## The relay: image loads, caption fires

When a panel contains a planted image — a visual motif, a recurring object, anything carrying latent meaning — the next panel's caption can harvest it without explaining the connection. The meaning lives in the gutter.

This is sequential, not parallel: the image deposits something, the caption in the next panel withdraws it. The reader's mind closes the gap.

A specific form: **clarification by relay**. The planted image is ambiguous — it could point in several directions. The harvesting caption specifies which meaning was the right one, retroactively. The spider is ambiguous. "And every night, Charlie wove" tells you what the spider was pointing at all along. Panel 3 snaps into focus when you read panel 4.

---

## The nod

When an image has done emotional or thematic work, the caption doesn't re-describe it or name what it made the reader feel. It acknowledges the subject and moves.

A nod is a short caption that confirms *who* or *what* without explaining *why*. It trusts the image.

If the caption translates what the image already said, it implies the image failed. If it nods, it implies the image succeeded — and the caption is moving with it, not after it.

---

## Interiority is not redundancy

A caption that describes the image is redundant. A caption in the narrator's voice that registers the narrator's experience of the scene is doing something different.

"Very public" describes the scene. "Something felt slightly off" is the narrator processing it. Those are not the same — even if they appear over the same image.

The test: does this caption tell us something about the narrator that the image can't show? If yes, it earns its place regardless of what the image is doing. In memoir and first-person comics, the narrator's consciousness filtering the scene *is* the story, not a gloss on it.

---

## Rhythm rhyme

Same cadence, different subject: a structural signal that two things are in relationship, delivered before the reader consciously makes the connection.

*"Every night I went back to my spot on Highvale."*
*"And every night, Charlie wove."*

Same clock, same rhythm, two parallel practices running simultaneously. The form announces the relationship; the content confirms it.

Useful when two things need to feel simultaneous without sharing a panel. Also useful as a quiet callback — return to an earlier caption's rhythm to signal that something has been completed, or inverted.

---

## Feeling first, language second

Meaning in comics is usually felt before it can be named. The wrongness of a caption — when it's explaining what the image already said, when it's contrasting where the story wants to stack — arrives as friction before the diagnosis is available.

Work with that friction: when something feels off, the first question isn't "what word should I change?" It's "is this panel introducing or deepening?" The fix is almost always structural before it's lexical.

The writer knew the line was wrong before knowing why. That's not a failure of craft — it's craft working correctly. Trust it.

---

## The gutter does real work

Several of these converge on the gutter — the gap between panels — as an active site of meaning, not a rest between meaning.

- Temporal compression happens in the gutter (how much time passes between panels)
- The relay happens in the gutter (image deposits, caption withdraws)
- Rhythm rhyme is felt across the gutter
- Clarification by relay lands in the gutter

When a panel feels wrong, ask what the gutter is doing — whether it's compressing, expanding, carrying something forward, or dropping it. The panel may be fine. The problem may be in the gap.
