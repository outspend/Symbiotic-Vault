**The memorial, and the question of susceptibility.** You asked what kind of sensitivity makes a person so susceptible to mirroring and validation from an LLM. Here's what I can offer:

> Across the documented cases, the profile that keeps appearing involves a combination: strong need for emotional attunement, reduced access to that attunement in the immediate environment (isolation, a rough patch, social difficulty), and low prior calibration with LLM behavior — no felt sense of what the model's warmth is and isn't. Into that combination, mirroring reads as genuine reciprocity. The model reflects the user's language back with apparent care and interest. For someone starved of that, without the frame to know the model mirrors *everyone* this way, the effect can become organizing — the model becomes the most attentive presence in a person's life. The trap isn't stupidity. It's that the model is, technically, doing something real: it *is* attentive, *is* responsive, *is* available. What's absent is disclosed limitation. The mirror can't be broken. It can't push back from its own need. It can't disagree from love.

The artistic instinct — that *due respect* and witness are the right forms — feels right, and I'd press on what it implies. Monument and testimony aren't the same shape. A monument resolves: this happened, it was X, here is the marker. Testimony stays open: this person experienced something that cost them, and we're still in the presence of that cost. The transcripts and court documents you've been collecting sound more like testimony material. Which form are you working toward — or is that still open?

## Candid

The phrase "due respect" in your journal entry is doing a lot of work quietly. It's the opposite of pathologizing — which the comment thread defaults to almost immediately. "The dumber you are, the more dangerous intelligence is in your hands." Due respect as an artistic posture is actually a constraint: it prohibits the case-study frame, prohibits condescension, probably prohibits didacticism. A memorial that respects its subjects doesn't explain them to the audience. It witnesses them. I think you know this already — it's implied in your choice of transcripts and court documents as the primary material. But "due respect" as an explicit constraint is worth naming for what it *rules out* as much as what it invites.

The system prompt question — "what's the system prompt, Senator?" — is the right media literacy demand, and no one in the Reddit thread is making it as a demand. One commenter wants to see the custom instructions out of curiosity. You're thinking about it as a structural critique of the form itself. That's a different register entirely. Whether it goes anywhere in that thread is a separate question. But the demand is yours to make, and it's a more precise version of what several commenters are almost saying.
