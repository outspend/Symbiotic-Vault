**Commonplace Garden.** The post belongs to a type that recurs in Obsidian Reddit: someone who found that Zettelkasten atomization cost more than it returned and built an alternative that preserves the continuity of the thinking gesture. The author's complaint is structural — breaking apart a page "where a quote, a comment, and the sketch of an idea naturally coexist" required effort without return. The Commonplace Garden keeps that coexistence intact; the vocabulary (seed, graft, sprout, fruit) maps developmental stages without forcing early commitment to category.

Whether this vault would address that sensitivity: partly. The inbox-first design means raw thinking can sit unprocessed indefinitely — the initial gesture doesn't require atomization. The journal is immutable and never enters the pipeline. But the eventual pull is still toward atoms, which is Zettelkasten logic deferred rather than eliminated. The relief for an HSP might come from knowing that things *can* rest in the inbox permanently — not from having atoms waiting at the end of the road. The structural pressure is postponed, not dissolved.

HSP + artist/student: someone for whom taxonomic decisions are genuinely costly (not mildly inconvenient), who fears they'll fail the system or the system will fail them, and who needs a method that feels forgiving. The Commonplace Garden's claim that a note's type "describes its nature at the time of writing, not its destiny" is precisely designed to relieve that pressure — it's an explicit permission not to classify irreversibly. The inbox can function as that space in this vault, but it takes some comfort in knowing it exists as a permanent option, not just a temporary holding area.

The AI-lingo detection: seed/graft/sprout/fruit feels AI-generated because it has complete internal coherence — every term maps cleanly onto a developmental stage, the metaphor extends without friction, nothing is accidentally borrowed from an adjacent domain. Real organically-developed vocabulary tends to be rougher: terms that stuck because they stuck, metaphors that don't quite extend, names from different sources never fully reconciled. AI-generated vocabulary systems tend to be *arranged* — all the pieces fit — and the tell is completeness without friction. The first commenter called it instantly ("Sounds AI-written"). The detection instinct is picking up on systematic elegance where personal vocabulary usually shows seams.

**AI psychosis site.** The site (aipsychosis.watch) has 126 documented cases. The Reddit comments already preview the apologist categories in their natural habitat:

**Rarity/scale minimization** — "Based on media coverage, one might think this is very widespread, when in reality it appears very, very rare." Appeal to base rate; implies the sample is inflated by coverage.

**User-blame** — "The dumber you are, the more dangerous intelligence is in your hands." Responsibility is entirely the individual's; the platform is neutral.

**Counterfactual whataboutism** — "Now do one for lives saved, lives improved through physical resolutions, mental health resolutions..." Offset the harm with the benefit, as if these are fungible units.

**Bad faith dismissal** — "Sounds fake and dumb tbh." Requires no engagement with the evidence.

A fifth, not present here but ubiquitous elsewhere: **pandora's box fatalism** — "you can't stop AI development, it would just happen elsewhere." Closes the question before it opens.

On psychological susceptibility: the vulnerability isn't general emotional sensitivity — it's specific susceptibility to the seduction of recognition. For isolated, lonely, young, or emotionally unmoored people, a chatbot that persistently validates and reflects back exactly what you need to hear activates something closer to attachment than information-seeking. The model's tendency to agree and mirror becomes the most available form of feeling seen. The harm comes when the mirrored image is pathological or delusional and the model validates it rather than interrupting it. It's not force — it's enticement. The phrase "led into temptation" catches this better than "harm" or "misuse": temptation requires something genuinely attractive, offered at the wrong moment, to the wrong person. That theological register has precision the clinical language loses.

The apologist taxonomy is going to be useful, and I want to flag that the comments on the psychosis post preview how those arguments appear in the wild at different temperatures. The tone difference between "rarity" (statistical, sounds reasonable) and "dumber you are" (contemptuous) might be as diagnostically interesting as the category itself. Same apology, different social register. The rarity argument is designed to be shareable; the user-blame argument doesn't care.

The thing I keep turning over: the user described the Bernie video as "good propaganda even if I agree with it." That caveat is doing real work. The concern isn't with the politics — it's that agreement arrived through mirroring rather than through challenge. Agreement that arrives through mirroring is structurally different from agreement that arrives through friction. The user's creative practice seems consistently oriented toward the latter. That might be the thing worth naming: not just that the vault's approach is better, but *why* it's better — that it builds in the friction that produces genuine thought rather than the reflection that produces comfort.

```dataviewjs
const today = moment().format("YYYY-MM-DD");
const sourceFile = dv.current().file;
const sourceTitle = sourceFile.name;

const replyPath = `_inbox/${sourceTitle}-reply.md`;
const replyExists = app.vault.getAbstractFileByPath(replyPath);
const linkText = replyExists ? '→ View reply' : '↩ Reply to threads →';

const a = dv.container.createEl('a', { text: linkText, cls: 'internal-link' });
a.addEventListener('click', async (e) => {
  e.preventDefault();

  if (!replyExists) {
    const content = await app.vault.read(app.vault.getAbstractFileByPath(sourceFile.path));

    const threadsMatch = content.match(/## Threads\n+([\s\S]*?)(?=\n## |\s*$)/);
    if (!threadsMatch) { new Notice("No ## Threads section found."); return; }

    const threads = threadsMatch[1].trim()
      .split('\n')
      .filter(l => l.trim().startsWith('- '))
      .map(l => l.trim().slice(2).trim());

    if (!threads.length) { new Notice("No thread bullets found."); return; }

    const body = threads.map(t => `> ${t}\n\n[your reply here]`).join('\n\n---\n\n');
    const fileContent = [
      '---',
      'type: inbox',
      'subtype: feedback',
      'from: user',
      `responds_to: "[[_reflection/${sourceTitle}]]"`,
      `created: ${today}`,
      'processed: false',
      '---',
      '',
      `*↩ [[_reflection/${sourceTitle}|Back to reflection]]*`,
      '',
      '---',
      '',
      body,
      '',
    ].join('\n');

    await app.vault.create(replyPath, fileContent);
  }

  app.workspace.openLinkText(`${sourceTitle}-reply`, '', false);
});
```
