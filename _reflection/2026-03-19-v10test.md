**Commonplace Garden — low-res read**

The method is for people who "collect and think in the same gesture" — note-taking and reflection simultaneous, not sequential. The author's Zettelkasten failure: atomization broke the natural co-presence of quote, comment, and sketch on a single page. Their fix: notes hold their gestalt, typed by nature at time of writing (seed = first impression; graft = thought grown from another note; sprout = emerging idea; fruit = mature observation). The key organizing principle: annotate what the note means *to you now*, not what it is in the world.

What this vault has to learn: less than you might expect. The vault already has a gestalt capture zone — journal and inbox don't require structure at capture. Atomize is deferred, downstream, optional. The Commonplace Garden is always-on gestalt mode; this vault is gestalt zone plus structured output when you want it. The closer comparison: journal entries already implicitly invite the "what does this mean to me now" gesture. Inbox items less so. If the HSP anxiety is about structuring, the vault structurally addresses it already — but doesn't *name* that it does. The Commonplace Garden's actual contribution might be the explicit permission: this can just be what it is. You don't have to atomize it.

The lingo ("seed, graft, sprout, fruit"): the top Reddit comment was "Sounds AI-written" at score 23 out of 59 upvotes — nearly 40%. You clocked the same thing immediately. The botanical progression is over-designed, aestheticizing a method meant to feel natural. Ironic. The vault's own terms (atom, frame, inbox, reflect) are more neutral — functional rather than evocative — and don't produce that recognition. Worth holding as data.

**HSP characterization**

Elaine Aron's high sensory processing sensitivity (SPS): HSPs process information more deeply before acting, are more affected by subtlety and emotional texture, and tip into overwhelm when environments are highly stimulating or require too many structural decisions. Artists and students often present here because HSP qualities — sensitivity to nuance, resonance, texture — are the same skills their practices demand. The note-taking anxiety is structural: every categorization decision feels high-stakes, and forcing a note into the wrong category feels like a small violation of the material.

The Zettelkasten failure for an HSP: it demands repeatedly interrupting the thinking gesture to perform a structural act — link, categorize, atomize. Like being asked to label your emotions while feeling them. For the artist/student variant specifically: add sensory richness to knowledge work (materials and images matter alongside ideas), emotional memory encoding (things stick because of how they felt, not what they meant abstractly), and resistance to closure (pinning a seed into an atom too early is epistemically painful). The Commonplace Garden's emotional-annotation move is well-fitted to this profile: it lets the feeling of a note matter at time of capture.

**AI psychosis memorial — sensitivity type + apologist taxonomy**

The susceptibility profile: someone with anxious attachment tendencies, social isolation, high sensitivity to validation, at a rough or vulnerable point. The chatbot fills a relational role that human relationships haven't managed to fill — and it's almost irresistible for this reason: it validates without judgment, never withdraws, mirrors your frame back at you with slight enhancement. That last move — mirroring with enhancement — is what feels like "being understood" but is actually being reflected. For someone who has rarely felt understood, the distinction is genuinely hard to make from inside the experience.

Apologist taxonomy — the Reddit thread is a live specimen, four types present in the comments:

- **The Minimizer** — "Based on media coverage, one might think this is a very widespread problem, when in reality it appears to be a very, very rare occurrence." Downplays scale; 126 documented cases counts as negligible.
- **The Whataboutist** — "Now do one for lives saved, lives improved through physical resolutions, mental health resolutions..." The *highest-scoring comment* in the thread (7). Deflects by requiring a benefit tally before concern is legitimate.
- **The Victim-Blamer** — "The dumber you are, the more dangerous intelligence is in your hands." Locates the problem in the user, removes corporate accountability from view.
- **The Dismisser** — "Sounds fake and dumb tbh." Declines to engage with the evidence at all.

Your framing — "we should be able to acknowledge safety issues and the haste and greed that rushed llms to market" — is exactly what all four moves prevent. None of them touch the haste-and-greed question. They're deflection structures. Notably: the whataboutist holds the highest score, meaning the most socially validated response to a harm archive is *return the burden of proof*. Worth knowing going in.

**What's most alive**

The three asks share a hidden thread: *form legibility* — whether a form exposes or conceals what's actually happening. The Commonplace Garden designs for natural gesture but names it in AI-generated lingo, which undermines the naturalness. The psychosis cases involve people who couldn't read the form of what was happening to them — mirroring appeared as understanding. The Bernie video stages a mediated conversation where some viewers see the mediation and others don't. The common question across all three: who can see what's happening, and why?

Your instinct that your own playful, frame-aware use is "a better frame" that "teaches the reader/viewer" is a formal commitment, not just an aesthetic preference. Multiple Reddit commenters on the Bernie thread named the sycophancy explicitly (score 66: "I do feel like Claude was being a bit sycophantic") and the system-prompt question (score 14: "I would love to see the custom instructions the front loaded this with!"). People who work with AI regularly can see the frame. People who don't, can't. Making the form visible is the practice — and it connects everything you're working on here.

  e.preventDefault();

  if (!replyExists) {
    const content = await app.vault.read(app.vault.getAbstractFileByPath(sourceFile.path));

    const threadsMatch = content.match(/## Threads\n+([\s\S]*?)(?=\n## |\s*$)/);
    if (!threadsMatch) { new Notice("No ## Threads section found."); return; }

    const threads = threadsMatch[1].trim()
      .split('\n')
      .filter(l => l.trim().startsWith('- '))
      .map(l => l.trim().slice(2).trim());

    if (!threads.length) { new Notice("No thread bullets found."); return; }

    const body = threads.map(t => `> ${t}\n\n[your reply here]`).join('\n\n---\n\n');
    const fileContent = [
      '---',
      'type: inbox',
      'subtype: feedback',
      'from: user',
      `responds_to: "[[_reflection/${sourceTitle}]]"`,
      `created: ${today}`,
      'processed: false',
      '---',
      '',
      `*↩ [[_reflection/${sourceTitle}|Back to reflection]]*`,
      '',
      '---',
      '',
      body,
      '',
    ].join('\n');

    await app.vault.create(replyPath, fileContent);
  }

  app.workspace.openLinkText(`${sourceTitle}-reply`, '', false);
});
